Section 01 · Industry 101

What is FMCG, actually?

FMCG — Fast-Moving Consumer Goods — is the industry of products you buy every week without thinking. Biscuits. Soap. Chips. Hair oil. Tea. Cola. The category sits on roughly 4–5% margin at the top, sells through ~12 million Indian retail outlets at the bottom, and runs on a single invisible engine: distribution.

A new D2C brand can be built with Instagram ads and a Shopify store. An FMCG brand cannot. To sell ₹1,200 Cr a year of biscuits in India, you need a distribution network that physically moves stock from your factory through ~800 distributors to ~4 lakh retail outlets, and refills each one weekly. The size and quality of that network is the moat. You can't beat HUL on distribution. You can only beat them on a category they're not focused on, in geographies where their network is thin.

The Indian FMCG market in numbers

The Indian FMCG market is roughly ₹6 lakh crore (~$72B) in 2025 and growing at ~9% per year. Three things shape it:

Scale

~12 million outlets

Indian retail is dominated by kirana stores — the corner shop. Modern Trade (organised retail like DMart, BigBasket, Reliance Smart) is still <30% of FMCG sales.

Geography

Rural is half

Roughly 40-45% of FMCG sales come from rural India. Reach is more important than depth in these markets — and the analyst's geographic lens is everything.

Mix shift

Premium + Health

Two long-running shifts: premium offerings (better quality, higher margin) and the health/wellness category (oats, nut bars, low-sugar). Both reshape the analyst's category mix.

General Trade vs Modern Trade — the eternal balance

Every FMCG analyst's life centres around this comparison. They are different worlds — different ordering patterns, different margins, different stakeholders, different data pipelines. Don't confuse them.

General Trade (GT)

  • Kirana stores — small, family-run corner shops
  • ~70-75% of FMCG volume in India today
  • Order-fulfilled by distributors, restocked weekly
  • Data is delayed 2-4 weeks (no daily POS)
  • Higher gross margins for the brand
  • Decisions: stocking, range, scheme, billing

Modern Trade (MT)

  • Organised retail — DMart, BigBasket, Reliance, Croma
  • ~22-25% of FMCG volume, growing fast
  • Direct billing to retailer chains, sometimes via DC
  • Daily POS data — much richer analytics
  • Lower margins (retailer takes more)
  • Decisions: assortment, planogram, listings, JBP
◆ Why this matters for you

The Bhārat Foods case has both GT and GT+MT distributors. The growth gap between them is one of the most important insights in the data — and one the NSD will be testing for in your deck. Modern Trade is growing roughly 3-4x faster than General Trade in this dataset. A junior analyst will report aggregate growth. A senior analyst will split the channels and tell the story of where the future is heading.

The two transactions that matter most

In FMCG analytics, every conversation eventually comes back to two transactions. Master these and you'll understand why the secondary-to-primary ratio is the single most important diagnostic in the Bhārat Foods case.

Sell-In

Primary Sales

Company → Distributor billing. This is what the company books as revenue. It happens when the distributor places an order. Easy to inflate — if the distributor agrees to take more stock than they need (often because they got a scheme discount), primary sales look great. But the stock isn't moving to consumers.

Sell-Out

Secondary Sales

Distributor → Retailer movement. This is the truer measure of consumer demand. If secondary is healthy, the distributor will reorder soon. If secondary is weak, the distributor's warehouse fills up — and they'll skip the next order, return stock, or pay late. This is the leading indicator the senior analysts watch.

★ The single most important sentence in this primer

When primary sales hold flat but secondary sales fall, you have a stocking-without-selling problem. The distributor's warehouse is filling up. Within 60-90 days, this almost always becomes late payment, returned stock, and eventual termination. The secondary-to-primary ratio falling below 0.70 is the FMCG equivalent of a fever — the body knows something is wrong before the symptoms become visible. Build your Distributor Health Score around this insight.

Why distributors exist (and why they're brutal to manage)

A first-time analyst often asks: "why doesn't the company just sell directly to retailers?" It's a great question with a complicated answer. Distributors exist because:

  • Geographic capillarity — Bhārat Foods can't physically have a salesman in every of the 50 cities and 4 lakh outlets. Distributors do that work locally.
  • Credit absorption — Distributors give 7-21 days credit to retailers. The brand bills the distributor and is paid in 30-45 days. Without distributors, the brand would need to be a bank.
  • Inventory holding — Distributors keep 21-30 days of stock locally. The brand doesn't need to maintain that working capital.
  • Local relationships — A distributor knows their kiranas personally. New product launches, schemes, and credit issues all run through that relationship.

But distributors are also a structural risk. They own the customer relationship with retailers. If a distributor fails or switches to a competitor, the brand can lose 6 months of presence in that geography. This is why distributor health is not just an analytics topic — it's an existential one.

Section 04 · Vocabulary

The words that separate you from a textbook analyst

Every industry has a vocabulary that immediately tells someone whether you're an outsider or an insider. FMCG is especially rich. Master these terms — every one will come up if you interview at HUL, ITC, Britannia, Dabur, Marico, or Tata Consumer.

The distribution vocabulary

  • Beat — A salesman's daily route through assigned retail outlets. Each PSR has 5-6 beats per week. A 'covered beat' = all planned outlets visited.
  • Outlet coverage / numeric distribution — % of reachable outlets that actually stock your product. Different from weighted distribution, which weights by outlet sales volume.
  • Range / Range-selling — How many SKUs each outlet stocks. A retailer stocking only your top SKU has poor range. A retailer stocking 8+ SKUs is a 'range-selling' outlet — much more valuable.
  • Depth of assortment (ECO) — Effective Coverage of Outlets. How many SKUs an outlet actually moves regularly, not just stocks.
  • MSL — Must Stock List. The minimum SKUs the brand expects every outlet of a certain class to carry. MSL compliance is a key audit metric.
  • Dispatch vs Billing — Stock physically leaving the depot vs invoiced. Quarter-end is famous for billing-without-dispatch (recognising sales without moving stock) — a common audit issue.
  • Damages / NRGP — Damaged stock returned. NRGP = Non-Returnable Gate Pass — the document that tracks it.

The trade-promotion vocabulary

  • Trade Margin — The percentage between billing price and MRP that the distributor + retailer share. Typically 15-20% of MRP for biscuits, 25-30% for snacks/HFD.
  • BTL (Below-the-Line) — Trade promotions, scheme spend, in-store activation. As opposed to ATL (Above-the-Line) which is mass media advertising.
  • BOGO — Buy One Get One. The most common consumer scheme.
  • Display Allowance — Cash paid to retailers for prominent placement (eye-level shelf, end-cap). A staple in modern trade.
  • Slotting Fee — Listing fee paid to modern-trade chains to introduce a new SKU. Can be ₹5L–₹50L per SKU per chain.
  • JBP — Joint Business Plan. Annual agreement between brand and modern-trade chain on volume targets, promotion calendar, listings, payment terms.
  • Trade-Promo ROI — Incremental sales generated ÷ scheme cost. Healthy is ≥2.5x. Below 1.0x = value destroyed.

The financial vocabulary

  • Gross Sales — Total billed value before discounts, schemes, returns.
  • Net Sales — Gross − discounts − schemes − returns. The number the CFO actually cares about.
  • Sec/Pri ratio — Secondary sales ÷ Primary sales. ≥0.85 is healthy. <0.70 is a red flag. The single most important leading indicator in FMCG.
  • Stock Days — Days of inventory at distributor level. 21-30 is healthy. 50+ is a warning sign.
  • Credit Period — Days the distributor has to pay. Typical: 21-45 days.
  • Overdue / DSO — Days Sales Outstanding. Average days a rupee billed waits to be collected. Higher = cash trapped at distributor level.

Section 05 · From business problem to data answer

The real questions FMCG leaders ask — and how an analyst translates them

The NSD doesn't walk in and ask "please calculate the secondary-to-primary ratio segmented by tier." They ask things like "are we burning cash on schemes?" Your job is to translate that into an answerable data question, run the numbers, and translate the answer back into a business decision. The Bhārat Foods case is essentially the first 5 of these, rolled together.

1. Business Problem

"Q2 looks good on top — is the growth real?" — NSD before a board review

Data Question

Decompose Q2 growth into category × channel × tier. Distinguish primary growth from secondary. Identify how much is propped up by trade-promo discounts.

Analytical Approach

Side-by-side primary vs secondary by category. Trade-promo dependency ratio (promoted volume ÷ total volume) per category.

Decision it Drives

How much of the headline number to celebrate. Whether to slow down promo spend in Q3.

2. Business Problem

"Which 50 distributors should I rescue and which 50 should I replace?" — RSM running a quarterly review

Data Question

Build a composite Distributor Health Score from 6-8 features (sec/pri, payment days, range-selling, returns, ASM cadence, training).

Analytical Approach

Weighted scorecard. Validate against historical 'bad distributor' outcomes. Tier them: Healthy / Watch / At-Risk / Critical.

Decision it Drives

Which distributors get reward, ASM intervention, credit hold, or termination notice.

3. Business Problem

"Are our trade promotions earning back?" — CFO before annual budget setting

Data Question

For every scheme run, compute incremental sales lift vs scheme cost. Distinguish forward-buying (volume that would have happened anyway) from genuine demand creation.

Analytical Approach

Baseline forecast vs scheme-period actuals. Per-scheme ROI. Distribution of ROI across scheme types and categories.

Decision it Drives

Which schemes to scale, redesign, or kill. Q3 promo budget allocation.

4. Business Problem

"Where do we put the next ₹40 Cr — defend or expand?" — CEO with quarterly capital to allocate

Data Question

Quantify defensive ROI (₹ exposure saved per ₹ of distributor remediation) vs offensive ROI (₹ market opportunity per ₹ of city expansion). Pick a split.

Analytical Approach

Defensive: Health Score outputs × intervention cost matrix. Offensive: City prioritisation using market size, current penetration, competitive intensity, MT share, feasibility.

Decision it Drives

How the company actually deploys ₹40 Cr in the coming quarter. Career-defining recommendation.

5. Business Problem

"This new launch is doing great — or is it?" — CMO defending Health Foods category investment

Data Question

Strip out trade-promo lift from launch growth. What's the underlying demand? What share of growth survives if promo is removed?

Analytical Approach

Promoted vs non-promoted volume comparison. Compare to category benchmarks. Test: cohort of outlets that received no scheme.

Decision it Drives

Whether to scale the category or restructure the launch. Career risk for the brand manager.

6. Business Problem

"Tier-2 and Tier-3 are growing faster than Tier-1 — where do we go deeper?" — Strategy lead

Data Question

Quantify the growth premium of Tier-2/3 vs Tier-1. Identify the cities where the gap is largest. Score each city on penetration headroom + competitive intensity.

Analytical Approach

City-tier growth comparison. City-level penetration analysis. Competitive intensity scoring. Feasibility filter.

Decision it Drives

Which cities receive new distributor appointments, sales-rep additions, market-development investment.

7. Business Problem

"Our Beverages numbers are off in the South — is it us or them?" — South Region RSM

Data Question

Is the dip widespread or concentrated? Which states show steepest declines? Is it primary or secondary that's falling? Could it be competitor activity?

Analytical Approach

State × month sales trends. Compare to other zones. Look for sudden discontinuities. Cross-check with returns and scheme data.

Decision it Drives

Whether to launch counter-scheme, do a market visit, or escalate to the CMO for brand-level intervention.

8. Business Problem

"Modern Trade is growing 3x faster than GT — should we lean in?" — CEO planning 5-year strategy

Data Question

What % of MT sales come through GT+MT distributors vs direct? What's the margin trade-off? Is the MT growth coming from new outlets or deeper share?

Analytical Approach

GT+MT distributor performance vs GT-only. Margin analysis by channel. New vs repeat MT account growth.

Decision it Drives

How much to invest in MT-capable distributor onboarding vs direct MT relationships. 5-year channel strategy.

Section 06 · The Role

A day in the life of an FMCG sales analyst

A composite Tuesday at the HQ of a mid-cap FMCG company. Almost none of this is the "machine-learning data scientist" work students imagine. Most of it is translation: fuzzy business questions in, sharp numeric answers out, business decisions back.

08:50
Open laptop. The morning sales-status email is already in: yesterday's primary billing was 4% below run-rate. Slack from the West RSM: "what happened?"
09:15
Pull yesterday's billing by zone and category. The miss is concentrated in West, in Beverages. Two large distributors didn't place their weekly order.
10:00
NSD's daily 10am call. "What's the West story?" You explain the two distributors. NSD: "Are they sitting on stock?" You check sec/pri ratio for both — yes, both at 0.55. "OK, get the ASM on a call. We're not pushing more stock until they sell what they have."
11:30
Build the monthly distributor scorecard for the upcoming Wednesday review. 800 distributors ranked on a composite score. Highlight: 47 distributors below 50/100 — the AT-RISK list.
13:00
Lunch with the Modern Trade team. They're asking for last quarter's MT growth split by chain — DMart vs BigBasket vs Reliance Smart. Promise to send by EOD.
14:30
Trade-promo review with CFO. The CFO wants to know which schemes from Q2 had ROI < 1.0x. Pull the analysis: 26 of 94 schemes were value-destroying, total ₹14 Cr wasted. CFO: "Why didn't anyone catch this in real time?" You explain the 30-day measurement lag. "Build me a leading-indicator dashboard."
16:00
Health Foods category head pings: "Need a story for the brand review. What's our growth in Tier-1 vs Tier-2/3?" You pull the cut. Tier-2 and Tier-3 are growing 4-5x faster than Tier-1 for Health Foods. Big news. You send the chart with three-line takeaway.
17:30
South RSM Slack: "Tamil Nadu beverages has dropped 42% in Sep. WTH?" You investigate. Check schemes in that state. Check competitor activity (informally — sales reps' notes). The pattern looks like a competitor scheme has gone live. Flag it back: "Pattern matches a competitive scheme. Suggest a market visit and counter-scheme proposal by Friday."
19:00
Send the EOD note to the NSD with three slides: West situation (two stocking distributors flagged), Q2 promo wastage (₹14 Cr to recover), Tamil Nadu Beverages (competitor pressure to investigate). Close laptop.
★ What you actually get paid for

Notice how much of the day is translation, not computation. The NSD doesn't want a chart — they want to know whether to get on a flight to West tomorrow. The CFO doesn't want a list of schemes — they want to know who's accountable for the wastage. The category head doesn't want a growth number — they want a story for their brand review. Great FMCG analysts don't just answer the question they were asked. They translate questions into decisions and decisions back into actions. Numbers tell you what happened. Business judgment tells you what to do. You need both.

Section 07 · The Language

KPI Dictionary — the words you must know cold

Every metric below shows up in the Bhārat Foods case. Master these before you touch the data. Click to expand.

Sales performance metrics

Primary Sales
Primary Sales (Value) = Σ (billed_units × billing_price)

Company → Distributor billing. The headline revenue number. Booked when the distributor places an order, regardless of whether retailers buy. Watch out for end-of-quarter inflation.

Benchmark: +9% YoY value growth for mid-cap FMCG.
Secondary Sales
Secondary Sales = Σ (cases moved from distributor to retailer)

The truer demand signal. When secondary tracks primary closely, the distribution channel is healthy. When primary grows but secondary doesn't, you have a stocking-without-selling problem.

Sec/Pri Ratio (Secondary-to-Primary Ratio)
Sec/Pri Ratio = Σ Secondary Sales / Σ Primary Sales (same period)

The single most important leading indicator in FMCG distribution. ≥0.85 is healthy, 0.70-0.85 is watch, <0.70 is red flag, <0.50 is critical. By the time payment days slip, this ratio has been falling for 60-90 days.

Benchmark: ≥0.85 healthy.
Range Selling (Active SKU %)
Range Selling % = SKUs actively sold by distributor / SKUs handled × 100%

Quality of distribution. A distributor handling 22 SKUs but only actively moving 9 has poor range. Predicts long-term distributor health better than total volume.

Benchmark: 65%.
Outlet Coverage (Numeric Distribution)
Numeric Distribution = Outlets stocking your product / Total reachable outlets × 100%

Quantity of distribution. Different from weighted distribution, which weights by outlet sales volume. A 65% numeric / 88% weighted distribution means you're in the 65% of outlets that drive 88% of category sales — the right ones.

Benchmark: 65% numeric for mid-cap FMCG.

Distributor health metrics

Average Payment Days / DSO
DSO = Σ (actual payment days × billed value) / Σ billed value

Days Sales Outstanding. How many days a rupee billed takes to come back. Direct cash-flow impact. Late payment is one of three classic FMCG distributor failure signals.

Benchmark: ≤ credit terms (typically 30 days).
Return Rate
Return Rate % = Return value / Primary value × 100%

How much stock comes back from distributor. High returns indicate near-expiry stock, quality issues, or — most often — distributor overstocking.

Benchmark: ≤ 3.5%.
Stock Days at Distributor
Stock Days = (Stock holding × 30) / Monthly secondary sales

How many days of inventory the distributor is sitting on at any point. 21-30 days is healthy. 50+ is a warning. 70+ means returns are coming.

Trade promotion metrics

Trade-Promo ROI
Trade-Promo ROI = Incremental Sales / Scheme Cost (multiple)

The headline ROI metric. Above 2.5x is healthy. Below 1.0x is value-destroying — you spent more on the discount than you earned in extra sales. Most FMCG companies have 20-30% of schemes in the value-destroying category at any time.

Benchmark: 2.5x; ≤25% schemes negative.
Incremental Sales (Lift)
Incremental Sales = Sales during scheme period − Baseline forecast

The 'extra' sales attributable to the scheme. The hard part is the baseline — it's the sales that would have happened anyway. Underestimating the baseline overstates the ROI. This is where most analyst careers get tested.

Promo Dependency
Promo Dependency = Promoted volume / Total volume (per category)

What share of a category's volume is moving on promotion. A 'healthy' brand has 15-25% dependency. A category with 50%+ promo dependency is structurally hooked — pull the promos and sales collapse.

Geographic and channel metrics

City-Tier Mix
Tier mix = Sales from each tier / Total sales

How sales split across Tier-1, Tier-2, Tier-3 (and rural for some companies). The growth premium of Tier-2/3 over Tier-1 in the last 5 years has reshaped FMCG strategy across the country.

Channel Mix (GT vs MT vs E-Com)
Channel Mix % = Channel sales / Total sales

India FMCG: ~70% GT, ~22% MT, ~5-7% E-Com. Mix is shifting toward MT and E-Com fast — and each channel has different margin economics. Your mix today predicts your margin tomorrow.

Modern Trade Growth Premium
MT Growth Premium = MT YoY % growth / GT YoY % growth

How fast MT is outpacing GT. Currently 3-4x in many categories. This single number is reshaping FMCG investment priorities — sales-org structure, distributor capability, JBP discipline, listing fees, the works.

Section 09 · Toolkit

The tools you'll actually use

Like every analyst role, the stated stack and the actual stack are different. Here's the honest breakdown by how much of your time each tool eats in an FMCG sales analytics job.

  1. Excel — 40-50% of your time. The bedrock. Pivot tables, SUMIFS, XLOOKUP, Power Query, weighted scorecards in SUMPRODUCT. Most FMCG executive reports still land here.
  2. SQL — 25-30%. Increasingly important as data warehouses replace flat-file SAP exports. Learn window functions, CTEs, and how to join 6+ tables.
  3. BI tool (Power BI / Tableau) — 15%. Most FMCG companies have weekly / monthly dashboards refreshed from the warehouse. You'll build and maintain.
  4. SAP / Oracle / DMS exports — 5-10%. Pulling raw data from the company's ERP is a specific skill. Lots of T-codes, weird formats, missing values.
  5. Python (pandas) — 5%. For the harder work — large dataset cleanup, statistical analysis, time-series forecasting. Not yet table-stakes at most Indian FMCG, but increasingly so.
  6. Storytelling — always. The analysts who grow fastest are the ones who can turn a 60-row pivot into a three-slide story the NSD can act on.
◆ The honest advice no one gives you

The FMCG analysts who get promoted quickest are not the ones who master the most tools. They're the ones who understand the field. Walk into a kirana store once a quarter. Visit a distributor's warehouse. Ride along on a salesman's beat. The patterns in your data make sense once you've seen the reality the data describes. Pure desk analysts plateau fast. Field-aware analysts become senior.

Section 10 · Approach

How to actually tackle the Bhārat Foods case

You now know enough context. Here's the sequence to follow if you were opening the workbook fresh. Don't be tempted to start on the Distributor Health Score — it'll be weaker if you haven't first understood the underlying performance story.

  1. Read the case doc twice, cover-to-cover. First pass: absorb the context and stakeholders. Second pass: underline each specific analysis question and required metric. Don't open the Excel yet.
  2. Write the executive summary you hope to end with. Before touching data, write your hypothesis. e.g. "Q2 grew 8% but the growth is mix-driven; Modern Trade is accelerating; Health Foods is propped by promo; 47 distributors are at-risk; recommend ₹22 Cr defensive + ₹18 Cr Tier-2/3 expansion". The gap between your hypothesis and what the data shows is where real insight lives.
  3. Clean the data — visibly. Unify date formats. Standardise state spellings. Resolve city variants. Handle \N/nulls. Document every cleaning rule. 8% of your grade lives here.
  4. Build the diagnostic views (Q1). Q2 vs Q1 by category, channel, zone, tier. Every view compared to benchmark, with green/amber/red flags.
  5. Promo ROI analysis (Q2). Per-scheme ROI. Negative-ROI count. By category, by mechanic. Identify the wastage.
  6. Distribution quality analysis (Q3). Outlet coverage, range-selling, payment behaviour, sec/pri ratios. The operational view before scoring.
  7. Build the Distributor Health Score (Q4 capstone). Features → weights → score → validate against bad-distributor outcomes → top-50 list → reasons → interventions.
  8. Build the strategic recommendation (Q5 capstone). Take Q4 outputs + Q1 diagnostic + city-demographics. Quantify defensive vs offensive ROI. Pick a split. Defend it.
  9. Synthesise → 3-bullet executive summary. Compare to your Step-2 hypothesis. The deck-narrative writes itself when steps 4-8 are done.
  10. Build the deck. 12-16 slides, no more. Every chart with a 'so-what' title. Every slide defendable.
  11. Sleep on it. Re-read. Cut 30%. The third draft is always the best.
◆ Final nudge

FMCG is unusual among industries — its complexity is hidden in plain sight. Every Indian has bought biscuits, but very few understand how those biscuits got onto the shelf. The analyst who studies the system, not just the spreadsheet, becomes invaluable. Do that. The Bhārat Foods case is your start. Good luck.
